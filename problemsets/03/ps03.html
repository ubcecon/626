Problem 1

(This was problem 3 last week)

Let \(X: \Omega \to \R\), \(W: \Omega \to \R\), \(Z: \Omega \to \R\), and \(D: \Omega \to \{0, 1\}\) be random variables. Let \(Y = D X + (1-D) W\). Suppose that \(Y\), \(D\), and \(Z\) are observed, but \(X\) and \(W\) are not.

  1. Suppose \(D\) is independent of \(X\), \(W\). Then show that \(\Er[X - W]\) is identified.

  2. Suppose \(Z\) is independent of \(X\) and \(W\), and \(\exists E_1, E_0 \in \sigma(Z)\) such that \(P(D=1 | E_1) = 1\) and \(P(D=0|E_0) = 1\). Then show that \(\Er[X-W]\) is identified.1

Problem 2

In the linear model, \(Y = \alpha + \beta X + \epsilon\), assume \(\mathrm{Var}(X)>0\) and \(\Er[\epsilon] = 0\). Show that without any more assumptions, \(\beta\) is not identified by finding values of \(\beta\) that are observationally equivalent.

Problem 3

Song (2021) Chatper 4, exercise 1.3.

Consider the binary choice model in Example 1.3 and assume that \(\tilde{\beta}_0\) and \(\tilde{\beta}_1\) have appropriate estimators \(\hat{\beta}_0\) and \(\hat{\beta}_1\). Provide a sample analogue estimator of the average derivative, replacing \(\tilde{\beta}_0\) and \(\tilde{\beta}_1\) with \(\hat{\beta}_0\) and \(\hat{\beta}_1\)

Footnotes

  1. An assumption like this is used in nonparametric selection estimators. One way to have such \(E_1\) and \(E_0\) is if \(D = 1\{Z \gamma \geq U\}\) and \(Z\) has unbounded supported. Then \(E_0\) and \(E_1\) are sets where \(Z \to \pm \infty\). Accordingly, this is sometimes called an identification at infinity assumption. See Heckman and Navarro-Lozano (2004) for more information, and a comparison between this case, and the (conditional) independence assumption of part 1.
